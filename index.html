Non- Invasive Glucose Monitoring System

This comprehensive review paper endeavors to quantify the concentration of glucose within the human body, a pivotal metric in ascertaining an individual's overall health.

Presently, the conventional approach to determining glucose levels involves invasive methodologies such as finger pricking, a methodology fraught with discomfort and impracticality, especially in emergency scenarios where swift and nonintrusive diagnostics are imperative.

This inherent limitation necessitates the exploration and implementation of non-invasive methodologies, which encapsulate an array of sophisticated sensors integrated into the measurement of glucose levels. By embracing non-invasive techniques, we seek to transcend the constraints imposed by invasive procedures, thereby enhancing the accessibility and expediency of glucose level assessments across diverse contexts and exigencies.

Design of Orthotic device for Knee Buckling

The design aims to provide support to the knee joint and prevent sudden buckling during activities of daily living. The literature review of this project report discusses the anatomy of the knee joint, the causes and consequences of knee instability, and the existing orthotic devices used for the treatment of knee buckling. The review reveals that there is a need for a customizable orthosis that can provide dynamic support to the knee joint and be comfortable to wear for extended periods.

The design methodology of this project report involves the use of integrating all the 5 methodologies together into a single device. The model was based on the anthropometric measurements of the lower limb of an average adult male. The orthosis was designed to fit snugly around the knee joint and provide dynamic support to prevent knee buckling. Further research can be conducted to optimize the design and test the orthosis on a larger population.

Design of Hemoglobin Meter

The study aims to introduce an accurate, cost-effective, and accessible diagnostic system based on the impedance measurement of RBCs. The core methodology involves exposing RBCs to a controlled electric field and monitoring their impedance, i.e., their resistance to the electric current. Results from the experiments confirm the potential of impedance measurement as a highly sensitive and specific means of distinguishing between anemic and non-anemic blood samples.

The technological components include the development of specialized equipment capable of capturing the impedance response of RBCs and the creation of software for data analysis. These innovations pave the way for a practical diagnostic tool for anaemia, which can be particularly valuable in low-resource healthcare settings.
